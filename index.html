Sales Call Script — River Pools Virginia

First call (qualify) → Assignment selling (homework) → The Zoom (deep conversation) → pay → site visit → design presentation (scripted later)

Stage 1 — The first call (phone)

Goal: confirm they're a real, good-fit buyer, then assign the homework and book the Zoom. Don't pitch, design, or quote. The lines in blue boxes are what you say; gold boxes are what you ask.

Open

"Hi [Name], this is [Sam] with River Pools Virginia — you reached out about a pool for your backyard. Did I catch you at an okay time for a couple minutes?"
"I won't keep you long — I just want to learn a bit about what you're after, make sure we're a good fit, and then get you set up for the next step. Sound good?"

Qualify — ask & listen

Ask:
  • "Whereabouts in Virginia are you?" (confirm it's in the service area)
  • "What's got you thinking about a pool this year?"
  • "What's your timing — this season, next year, or just exploring?"
  • "Have you thought about a rough budget for the whole project yet?" (light — just gauging fit)

You're deciding one thing: is this someone worth the team's time? If yes, move them to the homework + the Zoom. If they're not serious or way out of range, be honest and don't advance them.

Bridge to the next step

"Here's how we do this — and it's a little different from most builders. You're about to make a big investment, and I want you fully informed before we ever talk specifics. So I'm going to send you a couple of things to go through, and then we'll jump on a video call to go deeper on exactly what you want. That work for you?"

→ Now assign the homework (next tab) and book the Zoom for after they've gone through it.

Assignment selling — the homework

Do this at the end of the first call, once they're qualified. This is Marcus Sheridan's Assignment Selling: you require the buyer to consume specific content before the real conversation.

Why it works: a buyer who won't get educated isn't ready to buy — they're shopping on price alone. Educated buyers close far more often (Marcus's data: prospects who did the homework bought ~80% of the time vs ~25%). The homework both educates them and qualifies them, so the Zoom is a real conversation — not basic Q&A.

The 3 Ws — hit all three: Why it matters · What the assignment is · When it's due (and get a yes/no commitment — never "how's that sound?", which gets a meaningless "fine").

1. Why it matters

"Before we go deeper, here's how I work — you're getting ready to spend a lot of money, and I never want you to make a mistake on something this big. The best thing I can do for you is make sure you're fully informed first. So I'm going to send you a couple of things you're going to find really helpful."

2. What the assignment is

"First is a short video that walks you through exactly how a fiberglass pool gets installed, start to finish — so when we talk, nothing about the process is a surprise.

Second is our honest cost guide — so you can see what these projects really run and what moves the price up or down."

Keep it to about two pieces — don't overload them. Pull RPVA's real video + guide links from the card. [asset links on card] Topic order if you add more: how-it's-installed → cost → fiberglass vs concrete vs vinyl → after-care.

3. When it's due — get the commitment

"Will you take the time to go through both of those before our video call on [day]?"

Wait for a clear "yes." That "yes" is your first small agreement with them — and people who keep the small agreements keep the big ones.

Follow through

"Perfect — I'll text you the links right after we hang up, and I'll check in the morning of our call to make sure you got through them so we can hit the ground running."

If they didn't do the homework: gently push the call back until they have. The buyers who won't take time to get informed almost never buy — so this protects your time, too.

The Zoom — the deep conversation

This is Marcus's Pathfinder consultation, run over video. Its only job is to deeply understand what they want and why — not to design the pool or quote a price (that's a later call, after they pay and the site visit's done). They've done the homework, so skip the basics and go deep. Talk less, listen more.

How to run it — the Four Pillars:
  • Yes, And — receive everything with energy; never flinch at a concern; build the conversation forward.
  • Question first — ask, don't tell. When they push back, mirror their words and say "tell me more about that" instead of defending.
  • Law of Three — the first answer is never the full story. Go a few layers deeper until you hit real emotion or a clear picture.
  • Vanguarding — name the likely concern before they raise it.

Open — confirm the homework, then the Honest Agreement

Ask: "Before we dig in — did you get a chance to watch the install video and look through the cost guide?"
"Before we go further, I want to set this up right. You're making a big investment, and I know you don't want to get it wrong. So here's my promise: I'll be 100% open and honest with you the whole way — the good and the not-so-good, nothing held back. That's what you deserve. Fair?" (get a yes)
"And because it's such a big decision, I'm going to ask you some questions other builders won't — I really want to understand what's driving this for you before I say a word about pools.

It goes both ways, though: if I ever show you something you're not feeling, I need you to tell me straight. You won't hurt my feelings — it's the only way we get this right. Deal?" (get a yes)

The Foundational Five — ask, then listen

Go a few layers deeper on every answer ("Tell me more about that.") before moving on. Ask both people, by name.

1. The Past — their pool memory

Ask: "Before we talk about your backyard at all — tell me about your most memorable moment in a pool. Could be as a kid, as an adult, or watching your own kids. Who were you with? What were you doing? What made it special?"
Then go deeper
  • "What made that moment so special?"
  • "What do you remember feeling?"
  • "What pool memories do you want your own kids or grandkids to have?"

2. The Present — why now

Ask: "What's changed that makes now the right time to finally do this — versus last year, or next year?"
Then go deeper
  • "How long have you actually been thinking about it?"
  • "What finally pushed you to reach out?"
  • "What's the cost of not doing it this year?"

3. The Future — the perfect day (the money question)

Ask: "I want you to picture something. It's a Saturday next July — the pool's done, everything turned out exactly how you hoped. Walk me through the day. Who's there? Who's in the pool, who's on the deck? What are you doing? What do you see, hear, feel?"

Don't interrupt — let them paint it. Write down what they say word-for-word; you'll repeat it back.

Then go deeper
  • "What does this let you do that you can't do now?"

4. The Non-Negotiables — must-haves + why

Ask: "As you picture this, what are the absolute must-haves — the things that, if we couldn't deliver them, you'd walk away?" — then for each: "And why is that a must-have for you?"

Bonus questions (use if you sense there's more)

Ask: "What's your biggest fear or concern about doing this project?" · "How do you want this to change your life beyond just the pool itself?"

Play it back

"Here's what I'm hearing — [repeat their perfect-Saturday picture and their must-haves, in their words]. Did I get that right?"
"I'll be straight with you — we install fiberglass, so I'm biased. Where it wins is the years after: it goes in fast, the surface is smooth and resists algae, and it's the cheapest pool to own. Where it gives something up is fully custom shapes and going wider than about 16 feet. Based on what you told me, here's why I think it fits you…"
"You've seen our cost guide, so you've got a feel for the ranges. Does that feel right for what you're picturing?" (confirm the money fits the vision — don't quote a specific number here)

The next step — invite them to the design package

You're not closing the pool here. You're earning the next step: a paid design package, which triggers the site visit and leads to the design presentation later.

"So here's what I'd suggest as the next step. To turn everything you just told me into a real, accurate design and number for your exact yard, we do a paid design package — there are [two/three] levels: $500, $1,500, and $5,000. The fee is your first deposit toward the project. Once you're in, we come out and measure your property, build your actual design, and then get back together to walk through it. Looking at what you're after, most folks go with [X]. Which feels right to you?"

What each tier includes — and whether the fee credits toward the build — [pull from card].

If they hesitate: "Help me understand what's giving you pause there."

What to say — questions & objections

Hear them out, ask why first, then answer honestly. Never bash a competitor.

Price & budget

"It's more than we wanted to spend."

"'More than you wanted' — totally fair. Help me understand the number you had in your head?" [listen] "The cheapest quote almost never stays the cheapest once you see what's not in it. Can I show you what actually drives the price, and where we can flex to fit your world?"

"What does a pool cost?"

"I'll never dodge that. Most of our projects land between about $85K and $200K all-in, and luxury builds go higher — it depends on size, your yard, and what you add around the pool. Before I give you a real number, can I ask a few questions so it's accurate?"

"I got a cheaper quote from another builder."

"Good — you should compare. What's in their number? A lot of 'same pool' quotes are $30–$40K apart because one includes the patio, electrical, fence, and grading and the other doesn't. Send me theirs and I'll walk it with you line for line — no spin."
Fiberglass vs. concrete & vinyl

"Why fiberglass over concrete?"

"I'm biased — we install fiberglass. Concrete genuinely wins on one thing: a fully custom shape or an infinity edge. Where fiberglass wins is the years after — weeks to install not months, a smooth surface that resists algae, and the lowest cost to own. Concrete upkeep runs around $1,650 a year; fiberglass is a few hundred. What matters more — a fully custom shape, or lower upkeep and cost over time?"

"Isn't vinyl cheaper?"

"Upfront, usually yes. The catch is the liner — it needs replacing every five to nine years at a few thousand each. Fiberglass is more upfront but there's no liner and no resurfacing. It comes down to the first check versus the next twenty years."

"What actually is a fiberglass pool?"

"A one-piece shell molded in a factory and set into your yard finished — so the quality's controlled before it ever arrives, and it goes in fast. Concrete is built from scratch on site; vinyl is a frame with a liner that wears out."
Shape, size & custom

"I want a big, fully custom freeform shape."

"Help me understand what's driving that — the look, room for people, lap swimming?" [listen] "Honest limit: a fiberglass shell ships in one piece, so it tops out around 16 feet wide and you choose from molded models. A lot of the time we can hit what you're really after with the right model. If you truly want a fully bespoke shape, concrete is the better tool — I'll tell you straight."
Saltwater & maintenance

"I want saltwater so there's no chlorine."

"Common one — and I want to be straight: saltwater isn't chlorine-free. A salt system makes its own chlorine from the salt, continuously. What you really get is softer-feeling water and a lot less hands-on dosing — worth it for the feel and convenience, just not chlorine-free."

"How much maintenance is it?"

"Easier than concrete, but not zero. The smooth gelcoat resists algae — fewer chemicals, no resurfacing; many owners spend minutes a week. The one thing: fiberglass has its own water-chemistry rules, and following the care guide protects the finish and the warranty. I'll make sure you have it."
Equipment & automation

"Can I run it from my phone?"

"It can be set up that way — but it's an add-on, not automatic, and to switch a spa or waterfall from your phone the pool needs motorized valves built in. Tell me what you'd want to control from the couch and I'll design for it now instead of you finding out later it's manual."

"Can I get [specific equipment brand]?"

"Let me check what we install — most builders commit to one equipment family and service that one, so mixing brands isn't really a thing. What drew you to that brand? I'll show you the equivalent in what we run." [brand on card]
Timeline, mess & the yard

"How long does it take?"

"From permit to first swim it's usually a few weeks to a couple of months — I'll give you a real estimate once we've seen the yard. I'll never hand you a hard date I can't control; weather and inspections move things. I'd rather under-promise and beat it."

"Is this going to tear up my yard?"

"Yes — and I'd rather you hear it from me now. Heavy equipment, a big open hole for a while, and it looks worse before it looks better. People who know that going in ride it out and love the result. I'll walk you through exactly what to expect."

"Does the price include the patio, fence, and landscaping?"

"Let's be crystal clear so nothing surprises you. The pool price covers getting the pool in the ground. Things like the patio, electrical, fence, fine grading, and landscaping are often separate. I'll name every one and we'll confirm who's handling each." [inclusions on card]
Warranty, chemistry & cracks

"What's the warranty, and what voids it?"

"The big thing to know is what voids it: letting the water drop below the skimmer, skipping winterization, or bad water chemistry. Keep the water right and the finish and warranty are protected — and I'll get you the care guide." [lengths on card]

"Will my concrete patio crack?"

"Honestly — yes. Concrete cracks; it's normal on every patio as it cures. That's why we cut control joints, so it cracks where it's meant to instead of randomly. If you want zero cracks ever, concrete isn't the right material and I'll tell you that now."
Spa & covers

"Can I add a hot tub / spa?"

"What are you picturing it for — relaxing after work, the look with the pool, or serious hydrotherapy?" [listen] "If it's serious hydrotherapy, a standalone spa is usually better — more jets, more efficient, year-round. If it's the look and flow with the pool, an in-ground spa can be great. Let me match it to what you want." [spa stance on card]

"What about a cover?"

"Covers do real work — safety, heat, less cleaning. There's an automatic cover (built into the pool, has to be planned in now — hard to retrofit) and a safety cover for winterizing. Is your driver safety, season length, or less upkeep?" [options on card]
Financing

"Do you offer financing? / What's the monthly?"

"We do — most people finance a pool. You'd apply directly with a lender and I'll point you to the one we work with. I can't promise a rate, but as a rough idea, financing $100K runs around $950 a month over 15 years. Want me to connect you for real numbers?" [lender on card]
Stalling & the spouse

"We need to think about it."

"Of course — it's a big decision. Help me understand what specifically you want to think through — the design, the investment, the timing, or something I haven't covered well? If there's a question on your mind, I'd rather answer it now than have it sit."

"I need to run it by my husband / wife."

"Totally — and that's exactly why I like getting you both on together, so [name] hears it firsthand and can ask their own questions. Let's set 30 minutes with both of you — when works?"
Trust & "why you?"

"How do I know you'll communicate after I pay?"

"Fair question, and I won't dodge it — the most common complaint in this industry is builders going quiet after the deposit. So here's exactly how we stay in touch during and after your build, who your point of contact is, and what we do if something isn't right. Hold us to it." [after-care process on card]

"Why you over another builder?"

"I'd genuinely encourage you to get a few quotes. As you do, ask them the same questions I asked you today — and see if they take the time to understand what you actually want before they throw a number at you. You want the team that gets you and tells you the truth, not just the lowest bid."

"Do fiberglass pools pop out of the ground?"

"It can happen — but only when it's installed wrong, like bad backfill or draining it at the wrong time. Done right, it's a non-issue. That's why how it's installed matters as much as the shell — and it's a great question to ask any builder you talk to."

"Does a pool add value to my home?"

"It adds enjoyment value for sure. Resale is more it-depends — in the right neighborhood it helps, but I'd never tell you to buy a pool as an investment. Buy it for how you'll use it. What's drawing you to it?"

Marcus's script — straight from the book

Pulled verbatim from Marcus Sheridan's The 4 Pillars of Breakthrough Home Improvement Communication: The Pathfinder's Way — the pool-builder version of his Foundational Five, unedited. This is his in-home consultation script (kitchen-table framing and all); nothing here has been adapted.

Run it in order, top to bottom. Total time: 16–31 minutes.

Step 1 — Arrival & connection (5 min)

Greet warmly, light conversation, notice details about their home and family, set a friendly, professional energy.

Step 2 — Transition to the table (1 min)

"You know, before we walk through [the space/your property], I'd love to sit down here for about 15 minutes so I can really understand what you're trying to create. Is the kitchen table okay?"

Step 3 — The Honest Agreement (3–4 min)

"Mr. and Mrs. Johnson, before we get started, I'd like to address something very important with you…"
"This project is a big deal. You're looking at making a significant financial investment, and I know you don't want to make any mistakes…"
"For this reason, my commitment to you is that I'm going to be 100% open, honest, and frank — holding nothing back, as I'm sure that's what you'd want and I believe that's what you deserve, fair?" (wait for confirmation)
"And because this is such an important investment for you and your family, I'm going to ask you questions you haven't been asked by other contractors. In fact, before we even walk through your [space/property], we're going to sit down and take the time to really get to know what's driving this decision. Doing this will help ensure we create exactly the right solution for you. Can you do that with me?" (wait for confirmation)
"At the same time, just as I'm honest and open with you, it's imperative you're honest and open with me. I promise, you will not hurt my feelings by always telling me the truth. For example, when I show you something today about this project you don't like, what do you need to do?" (they should say: "Tell you" or "Be honest")
"That's right, because otherwise, we'll make mistakes. Therefore, will you commit to being completely open and honest with me about your feelings regarding this important project?" (wait for a verbal "yes")

Pause after each part, make eye contact, let it land. Never say "How's that sound?" — you'll get "fine," which isn't a commitment. Don't move on without a clear "yes."

Step 4 — Question 2: The Past (3–5 min)

"Before we talk about your backyard, I want to understand something. Tell me about your most memorable moment in a swimming pool. It could be when you were a kid, or as an adult, or watching your own kids. Who were you with? What were you doing? What made that moment special?"
Then go deeper — Law of Three
  • "What made that moment so special?"
  • "Who was with you?"
  • "What do you remember feeling in that moment?"
  • "When you think about your own kids/grandkids, what pool memories do you want them to have?"

Take notes — you'll repeat this back to them later.

Step 5 — Question 3: The Present (3–5 min)

"You've been thinking about this for [X time]. What's changed that makes NOW the right time to finally do this project?"
— or —
"Tell me, what made you want to do this project at this point in your life?"
Then go deeper — Law of Three
  • "How long have you actually been thinking about this?"
  • "What finally pushed you to pick up the phone and call?"
  • "What's the cost of not doing this project this year?"

Step 6 — Question 4: The Future (4–6 min)

"I want you to imagine something for me. Close your eyes if it helps. It's [6 months/a year] from now. This project is complete. Everything turned out exactly as you hoped — maybe even better. Walk me through a perfect [morning/Saturday/evening] in this space. Who's there? What are you doing? What do you see, hear, feel? Give me as much detail as you can."
Pool version: "It's July next year. The pool is done. It's a Saturday afternoon. Describe what's happening. Who's in the pool? Who's on the deck? What are you doing? What makes it feel perfect?"

Don't interrupt — let them paint the whole picture first.

Then go deeper — Law of Three
  • "What does this space allow you to do that you can't do now?"
  • "What's the vibe? What's the energy?"
  • "What sounds do you hear? What do you see?"

Step 7 — Question 5: The Non-Negotiables (3–7 min)

"As you think about moving forward with this project, what are the absolute must-haves? What are the things that if we couldn't deliver them, you'd walk away and say 'this isn't going to work'?"
For each one: "And why is [that specific thing] a must-have for you?"

Step 8 (optional) — Bonus questions (2–4 min)

Use if you sense there's more:

The Frustration: "What's been the biggest frustration with your current [lack of pool], and how do you want to avoid that in what we create?"
The Fear: "When you think about doing this project, what's your biggest fear or concern?"
The Impact: "How do you want this space to impact your life beyond just the physical transformation?"

Step 9 — Stand up & transition

"Perfect. Now that I understand what you're trying to create and why this matters so much, let's walk through the space together. I'm going to see it completely differently now that I know your vision."

Moves to use anytime (the Four Pillars)

These aren't steps — use them whenever they fit.

Pushback Pivot — when they push back, mirror their exact words, then "tell me more about that":

  • "Expensive… please… tell me more about that."
  • "Bigger design for less money? Tell me about what they showed you."
  • "No more than $50,000? Thanks for sharing that. Help me understand how you arrived at that number."

"But if you had to choose…" — for a vague, non-answer:

"But if you had to choose an answer, what would it be?"

Vanguard the spouse who isn't present:

"I notice your husband (or wife) isn't here today. That's totally fine, but here's what I've learned over the years: if he's not part of this process — if he doesn't hear your vision, doesn't understand why this matters, doesn't see the design options — there's a good chance this won't move forward, even if you love what we create. I've seen it happen too many times. One spouse is excited, the other feels blindsided by the cost or the plan, and the whole thing stalls. How involved does he (or she) need to be in this decision, and should we wait for him to join us before we go too far down the road?"

Vanguard your premium pricing position:

"Before we talk about investment, I want to be transparent about something. We're not the cheapest option you'll find — not even close. If lowest price is your primary criteria, we're probably not the right fit, and that's okay. What we are is the best value for homeowners who care about quality, communication, and a process that actually delivers what was promised…"

Vanguard hidden costs / contingency:

"One thing I've learned after [X] years in this business: almost every project uncovers something unexpected once we dig into the yard. It's not a matter of if we'll find something — it's usually a matter of what we'll find. I always recommend having a 10–15% contingency in your budget for these surprises. Would you rather know about that possibility now and plan for it, or be shocked and stressed when it happens mid-project?"
